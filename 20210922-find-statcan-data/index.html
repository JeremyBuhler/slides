About Statistics Canada

Serving Canada with high-quality statistical information that matters

"Statistics Canada produces statistics that help Canadians better understand their country - its population, resources, economy, society and culture."

from https://statcan.gc.ca/about/about

A few words about Statistics Canada. Its mission is to collect and provide data about Canada's population, resources, economy, society, and so on.

But serving Canada does not necessarily mean making the data available to everyone. In fact, withholding some of this data from public use is part of Statistics Canada's service: preserving the privacy of people who respond to the census or to surveys, for example.

Statistics Canada Open Licence

https://statcan.gc.ca/reference/licence

Grants permission to

  • use
  • reproduce
  • publish
  • freely distribute
  • sell

Though it can't release all the data it collects, there is a subset of data that Statistics Canada does release for public use under the terms of thee Statistics Canada Open License.

This is quite a permissive license that allows anyone to "use, reproduce, publish, freely distribute, and even sell" the data and products derived from it, with the conditions that it not be used to try to identify individuals, and that Statistics Canada is properly credited as the data source.

So what kind of data is out there and how does it get used?

Chances are you've come across news stories like this one from CBC that cite Statistics Canada as its source. This story is a fairly high level overview that provides some recent statistics about current employment in Canada compared to before the pandemic. It states that the overall unemployment rate in August 2021 was 7.1%.

This might be useful in your research if the news story has exactly what you're interested in, but what we see in this story are statistical summaries at a very high level: they're easy to understand but don't provide much nuance.

Statistical overview
  • counts, percentages, etc. for certain regions or groups
  • calculated for a purpose, but not very flexible
Underlying data can answer follow-up questions

How does that differ by province? By age group?

Statistics Canada produces many tables that provide statistical overviews with counts and percentages. These data tables are often freely available on the Statistics Canada website and may have filters that allow you to adjust the parameters to a limited degree. The tables are calculated for a particular purpose, however, and are not very flexible if you happen to be interested in variables or population characteristics that weren't included in their design

In many cases, Statistics Canada also provides access to the underlying data to meet this kind of need, so you can answer follow-up questions and dig deeper into patterns that are not represented in the published statistical overviews.

Public Use Microdata Files (PUMFs)

Public data modified to protect respondent privacy

  • Some variables suppressed
  • Some variables aggregated
  • May only be a sample of responses

That's where the Public Use Microdata Files, or PUMFs, come in. Please use the reactions button to indicate yes or no, whether you've looked at PUMF data before. Even if you've just opened a PUMF to see what's inside, answer "yes"; if you you've never accessed a PUMF, answer "no". This workshop doesn't assume any prior experience with PUMFs, but it will help me to understand what experience you have.

PUMFs are files that contain case-level data from the Census and from Statistics Canada surveys, meaning each row contains data for one individual. There's a lot you can do with this level of detail, but researchers should be aware that this public data is modified in a few ways to protect respondent privacy. Sensitive variables or those that could identify individuals may be removed from the PUMF or aggregated. For example, PUMFs don't usually contain sexual orientation data, even where that data is collected, and age might be reported in age groups instead of an exact age. For the Census, the PUMF represents a 2.7% sample of the Canadian population - not responses for every individual. This is still plenty for of data for many kinds of research, but those who need data not included in the PUMFs may need to apply for access to a Research Data Centre, as the more detailed data is not freely available.

Steps for Statistics Canada Data search. make sure window is sized so subjects appear on left, not top

  • Orient, show subject list
  • Filter by subject "Labour", note large number of results
  • Search keywords "employment"
  • Switch to "Tables" tab, select "Labour force characteristics by province..." in new tab
  • Point out the 7.1% unemployment rate from article
  • Scroll back up, show filters and add/remove data; but what if you need even more detail?
  • Scroll to bottom, show source of data (Labour Force Survey)
  • Return to search results
  • Switch to PUMF tab and select LFS (second in list)
  • Note data availability, indicated by links (older data not available)
  • Two reasons to go to Abacus: PUMF exists but is not accessible from site, or PUMF is there but want more documentation and related files, or data in a different format (LFS is only in CSV on Statistics Canada site, but SPSS file in Abacus)

Steps for Abacus Open Data collection

  • Orient, show filter options
  • Note that most recently updated datasets appear first
